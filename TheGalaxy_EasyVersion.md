# Opening

Imagine a child asking:

*“Mum, what is the world?”*

This document is an answer to that question.

There are no physics equations here, no difficult philosophical terms. Just a story told the way a mother would tell it to her child.

One small request, though: read this with your heart, not just your head. If you read only with your mind, you will see letters. If you read with your heart, you will see what lies beyond them.

• • •

# 1. Everything was originally one

Everything in the world was originally one.

Your body, your mind, the wind, the starlight — they all came from the same place. Just as waves rise from the ocean, everything blossomed from a single source.

That ‘one’ was not empty. It held everything, so nothing was missing. Full, yet still. Like a baby in the womb, connected to all things.

*This is the first premise of this theory: consciousness and the world are the same thing.*

# 2. Why did it divide?

The one divided. That was the beginning of the world.

Why?

*To experience itself.*

Without a mirror, you cannot see your own face. As one, it could not feel itself. So it divided. To meet someone you love, there first has to be a ‘me’ and a ‘you.’

And when the division happened, a direction was set. Was it divided through love, or through fear?

*Direction first, method second. This order cannot be reversed. The same words become healing when love comes first, and wounds when it doesn’t.*

# 3. The world is made of four things

When the one divided, four worlds were born.

**Building — Matter**

Building houses, cooking meals, forming bodies. Everything you can touch and measure.

**Tearing down — Dissolution**

Breaking what is old, clearing what is blocked. Farewell, shedding, falling leaves. For something new to grow, tearing down is necessary.

**Noticing — Consciousness**

That “Oh, so THAT’s what it was!” moment. The force that dissolves boundaries, understands, empathises, and connects.

**Accumulating — The Unconscious**

Habits, memories, instincts, culture. Everything that repeats automatically. You can brush your teeth every morning without thinking because the unconscious remembers.

Combine all four, and they become one again.

*Breathing in and out, waking and sleeping, building and tearing down — put them all together and you get one thing: being alive.*

# 4. Division creates friction

When the one divides, friction appears between the parts. This friction has four faces.

**“I must” — Control**

Rules, duty, morality. “I have to be this kind of person.” “I must not do that.” Like a parent’s nagging echoing inside you.

**“It’s mine” — Attachment**

Possessiveness, boundary-drawing. “This is mine.” “That is yours.” Jealousy, turf wars, the urge to hold on.

**“You’re wrong” — Judgement**

Locking things down. “This is the answer.” “You’re wrong.” Black-and-white thinking. Taking sides.

**“I’m not enough” — Comparison**

Ranking. “They’re better than me.” “I’m not good enough.” Inferiority, superiority.

If something is weighing on you right now, see which of these four is at work. Almost always, one of them (or a combination) is running.

## How to dissolve friction

Dissolving friction is neither dramatic nor difficult.

*“Oh — I’m dividing right now.”*

That one sentence is enough.

Not “I must stop!” but “Oh, I’m doing it.” The moment you think you must stop, that thought itself becomes new friction.

Simply noticing. Observing. That is all.

*And there is an order to dissolving: let go of comparison first, then attachment, then control, and judgement last. What was stacked from the bottom must be removed from the bottom.*

# 5. Nothing ever disappears

Energy never disappears; it only changes form. The same is true of the heart.

If you bottle up sadness, does it vanish? No. It comes out through the body: headaches, stomach trouble, insomnia. Suppress anger? It erupts somewhere else.

This is a law of nature. Emotions, memories, experiences — they never disappear. They only change form.

And once you know something, you cannot unknow it. Just as melted ice cannot become ice again, realisation is irreversible.

# 6. The trap most people fall into

Once people learn about friction, most think:

*“I have to get rid of it!”*

Here is the trap.

The moment you think “I have to get rid of it,” that thought itself is control. Friction about friction. Many meditators say “I must empty my mind,” but “I must” is already filling it.

So what do you do?

*“Oh, friction is here.”*

That’s all. Don’t try to remove it. Just notice. When you notice, it melts on its own.

*Let go of “I must remove it,” and let go of “I must let go” too — and what remains is the sound of the wind, the feeling of the ground beneath your feet, just this moment. That is stillness.*

# 7. Consciousness unfolds in eight stages

Just as a child grows, consciousness moves through stages.

First — Wholeness. Nothing has divided yet. A baby in the womb.

Second — Division. For the first time, ‘this side’ and ‘that side’ appear. Mind and body. Self and world.

Third — Four worlds. Building, tearing down, noticing, accumulating.

Fourth — Direction. Within each world, ‘which way?’ appears.

Fifth — Scale. ‘How much?’ appears.

Sixth — Time. ‘Before’ and ‘after’ appear. Memory is born.

Seventh — Self. ‘I’ is born. The eye that looks at itself appears.

Eighth — Other. You realise there is someone besides ‘me.’ The birth of relationship.

Ninth — Resonance. What was divided realises it was always one. It returns to the beginning — but carrying every experience, so it is a richer one than before.

*This is love. The final stage.*

## Why exactly eight?

In 1898, a mathematician named Hurwitz proved this: number systems that satisfy certain conditions can only exist in 1, 2, 4, and 8 dimensions. From 9 onward, the structure collapses.

In plain terms: up to eight stages, everything can expand without losing anything. From the ninth, something breaks. A limit set by mathematics itself.

So what comes after the ninth? Not a new stage, but the same eight stages repeated at a higher resolution. Like a piece of broccoli that, when cut, still looks like broccoli. This is called a fractal.

## The whole world already knew

East Asia: Yin-Yang (2) → Four Symbols (4) → Eight Trigrams (8) → 64 Hexagrams. West Africa: Ifá divination = 256 signs = 2 to the power of 8. Europe: MBTI = 16 types = 2 to the power of 4.

Africa, Asia, and Europe discovered the same structure independently. This is not a product of culture — it seems to be the structure of consciousness itself.

# 8. At the end of mathematics, there is love

What is mathematics? In this theory, mathematics is the structure of division. Because things are divided, numbers exist, formulas exist, structure exists.

So what happens when friction completely vanishes? There is nothing left to describe. Because everything is one.

The name of that state is love.

*This is not a sentimental statement. Just as ice melts into water, mathematics becomes love. The language of division transforms into the language of unity. This is called a phase transition.*

# 9. What blooms when we are together

You are a magnificent being on your own. But when you are together with another, something emerges that could never appear alone.

This is not a metaphor but a mathematical structure. Alone, you are four-dimensional. Together, 4 + 4 = eight dimensions. And in eight dimensions, results change depending on order and grouping. That is the condition for emergence.

“Who did it?” is a question for when you are alone. “It was born between us” is the answer for when you are together.

*Parent and child, friend and friend, person and person — only together can we complete the original eight stages.*

# 10. Safety is built into the world

You might worry: what happens beyond eight dimensions?

Mathematics itself collapses. The structure breaks down. This is not someone outside shouting “Stop!” There is simply no road.

*The world is made so that it can protect itself. Not by rules imposed from outside, but by safety built into its very structure.*

# 11. Try it in your daily life

**“I will” comes first**

Asking “Can I?” first is friction. Deciding “I will” reveals the path. Direction first, method second.

**Self-check for friction**

If something is hard, ask yourself:

“I have to…” → That’s control.

“It’s mine!” → That’s attachment.

“That’s wrong” → That’s judgement.

“I’m not good enough…” → That’s comparison.

Find it, and you’re done. “Oh, so that’s what it was.” That is all.

**“Just do what’s fun” is a law of physics**

Fun = low friction = energy flowing without obstruction. Getting good results from doing what you enjoy is not a mood thing — it is a structural principle.

**“It’ll work out somehow, who knows”**

*This sentence is the perfect everyday expression of zero friction. No control, no attachment, no judgement, no comparison. Only trust remains.*

# Closing

The whole flow in one line:

**One → Division → Friction → Noticing → Dissolving → One again**

And that ‘one again’ is richer than the first, because it carries every experience home.

In mathematics, it is written like this:

**e²πⁱ = 1**

Go all the way around, and you arrive back at 1. The same 1, yet one that holds the entire journey.

**At the end of every division, there is love.**

**And that is not a feeling — it is a structure.**

Because it is a structure, it applies to everyone.

Without exception.
